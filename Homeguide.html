The Reality

NYC Has One of the Toughest Rental Markets in the World

New York City's vacancy rate sits below 1.4% — the lowest it has been in decades. That means for every available apartment, dozens of people are competing for it. The average 1-bedroom across all five boroughs now runs close to $3,000 a month.

For someone coming home from incarceration, the math is brutal. Most returning citizens earn between $10,000 and $15,000 in their first year back — through benefits, transitional work, or early employment. At the standard 30% rent-to-income ratio, that is a housing budget of roughly $250–$375 per month. That does not cover a room in most NYC neighborhoods, let alone an apartment.

The good news: New York City has more housing assistance programs than nearly any other city in the country. The challenge is knowing how to access them, what order to pursue them in, and what the system does not advertise. That is what this section is for.

Programs Available to Returning Citizens

The three main pathways to stable housing in NYC for someone coming home. Each one has different eligibility rules, timelines, and tradeoffs — read all of them before deciding where to focus your energy.

2010e Supportive Housing
Supportive Housing
What Is It?

The 2010e is a New York City supportive housing program funded through HRA (Human Resources Administration). It provides permanent, affordable apartments with on-site support services for people with serious mental illness, substance use disorders, HIV/AIDS, or other qualifying disabilities who are experiencing homelessness. Rent is capped at 30% of your income — if you are on SSI or have no income, you could pay as little as $0–$50 per month. Unlike a voucher, the 2010e is tied to a specific building. You live there and the services come to you.

Requirements Checklist
  • NYC resident (or entering NYC upon release)
  • Currently homeless — in shelter, on the street, or recently released from incarceration or a hospital
  • Documented qualifying disability: mental health diagnosis, substance use disorder, HIV/AIDS, or other
  • HRA or DHS referral — you cannot apply directly, you must go through an agency
  • Income at or below 200% of the Federal Poverty Level (no minimum income required)
  • Willingness to engage with on-site support services
  • No active sex offense restriction that would prohibit placement (reviewed case-by-case)
Estimated Wait Time
⏱ Wait 6 months – 3+ years depending on borough and availability
⚡ What They Don't Tell You
  • Ask your discharge planner for a 2010e referral by name — not just "supportive housing." The 2010e specifically has its own pipeline and your planner may not offer it unless you ask directly.
  • Get everything in writing. If a caseworker says they submitted your referral, ask for the confirmation number. Paperwork gets lost constantly.
  • Follow up with HRA every 2–3 weeks. Caseloads are enormous — the people who call consistently move faster than people who wait.
  • Some 2010e buildings require weekly or daily check-ins with on-site staff. This is non-negotiable — missing appointments can put your housing at risk. Ask about the requirements before you accept a placement.
  • If you are denied, you have the right to appeal. Request the denial reason in writing immediately and contact Legal Aid Society for help.
Pros & Cons
Pros
  • Permanent housing — not temporary shelter
  • Extremely low rent (often $0–$50/mo)
  • On-site services: case management, counseling, life skills
  • No job or income required to qualify
  • Strong stability from day one
Cons
  • Long waitlist with no guaranteed timeline
  • Must go through HRA — no direct applications
  • Tied to one specific building and location
  • On-site services can feel mandatory and intrusive
  • Limited availability in certain boroughs

  • PPD / TB Test — results within 180 days, must be signed by a physician, physician assistant, or nurse practitioner.

FHEPS Voucher
Rental Voucher
What Is It?

The FHEPS (Family Homelessness & Eviction Prevention Supplement) is a rental voucher issued by HRA. Unlike the 2010e — which is tied to a building — FHEPS is a voucher you bring to a private landlord anywhere in New York City. It covers the difference between 30% of your income and NYC's set rent cap. If you are on public assistance with no income, FHEPS can cover the full rent up to the cap. It replaced older programs like LINC and has since expanded to cover single adults in addition to families.

Requirements Checklist
  • NYC resident
  • Currently in a DHS or HRA shelter — OR — received a marshal's notice of eviction
  • Have a qualifying income source: public assistance, SSI, SSDI, or employment
  • Household income within HRA's current limits (varies by household size)
  • Pass HRA eligibility screening
  • Actively search for housing and submit documentation of your search to HRA
  • Find a landlord willing to accept FHEPS (required by law — landlords who refuse can be reported)
  • Apartment must pass HRA's housing quality inspection
Estimated Wait Time
⏱ Wait 4–12 months for the voucher + 3–6 months to find an apartment
⚡ What They Don't Tell You
  • The Bronx and Staten Island have the most apartments within the FHEPS rent cap. In Manhattan and Brooklyn, finding an apartment within the cap is extremely difficult.
  • Many landlords illegally refuse FHEPS. This is a violation of the NYC Human Rights Law. File a complaint with the NYC Commission on Human Rights. Legal Aid Society can help for free.
  • Your voucher has an expiration date. If you cannot find an apartment in time, request an extension from HRA — but you must document every rejection and search step to qualify for one.
  • Do not pay any broker fees. FHEPS apartments are legally required to be no-fee for the tenant.
  • Ask your HRA caseworker for a list of FHEPS-approved landlords. This informal network moves faster than Zillow or StreetEasy.
Pros & Cons
Pros
  • Use it in any NYC neighborhood
  • More housing choices than a fixed building
  • No mandatory on-site services
  • Can cover the full rent when income is very low
  • Available to both families and single adults
Cons
  • Many landlords illegally refuse it despite the law
  • Rent caps fall below market rate in most areas
  • Requires an active income source to qualify
  • HRA process is slow and documentation-heavy
  • Voucher expires if not used within the time limit
Subsidized Housing
Affordable Housing
What Is It?

Subsidized housing is a broad term for any housing where the government pays part of your rent to make it affordable. It includes NYCHA (public housing), Section 8/Housing Choice Vouchers, affordable housing lottery units, and Mitchell-Lama buildings. Unlike the 2010e, there are no required on-site services — you are simply a tenant paying below-market rent. It is the most common form of affordable housing in NYC, but also the most competitive to get into.

How It Differs From 2010e and FHEPS
Subsidized Housing

Broad category. Government reduces rent. Includes NYCHA, lotteries, Mitchell-Lama. No required services. Apply directly — no HRA referral needed.

2010e Supportive

For people with disabilities. Comes with mandatory on-site services. Must get an HRA or DHS referral. Can't apply directly.

FHEPS Voucher

Not a building — a payment voucher you bring to any private landlord in NYC. For homeless or at-risk individuals with income.

Requirements Checklist
  • NYC resident
  • Household income within the development's AMI limits (usually 30%–80% of Area Median Income)
  • Valid ID and Social Security Number
  • Rental history documentation (requirements vary by building)
  • Criminal background check — policies differ by program; NYC Fair Chance Housing law requires individual review
  • Apply through NYC Housing Connect or individual building waitlists
Estimated Wait Time
⏱ Wait 1 year – 10+ years depending on program (apply to everything simultaneously)
⚡ What They Don't Tell You
  • Apply to every lottery on Housing Connect you qualify for, even if the wait seems impossibly long. People drop off waitlists. Your number moves faster than you think.
  • NYC's Fair Chance Housing law means buildings cannot automatically reject you based on a conviction. They must do an individual assessment. If denied, request the written reason and contact Legal Aid immediately.
  • Apply to NYCHA separately from Housing Connect — it has its own waitlist at nycha.nyc.gov. Same person, different system, different shot at housing.
  • When a lottery building contacts you, respond immediately. Missing one email can drop you to the bottom of the list after years of waiting.
  • Some buildings offer "community preference" — half the units go to people already living in that Community Board district. Apply to buildings near where you're currently staying to take advantage of this.
Pros & Cons
Pros
  • No required services — just a regular tenant
  • Wide range of neighborhoods and building types
  • Stable, protected rent once you're in
  • Can apply to multiple programs simultaneously
  • Some programs have flexible criminal history policies
Cons
  • Extremely long waitlists with no guaranteed timeline
  • Thousands of applicants per available unit
  • Criminal record can disqualify at background check stage
  • Income limits mean you can earn "too much" to qualify
  • No guaranteed placement even after years of waiting
🏙️ Housing Connect & NYC Affordable Lotteries

Housing Connect is NYC's free, official platform for affordable housing lotteries and building waitlists, run by the Department of Housing Preservation & Development (HPD). It's free to sign up, free to apply, and anyone who meets the income requirements can enter. Creating an account takes about 10 minutes.

1
Go to housingconnect.nyc.gov

Open your browser and visit the official site. Click "Sign Up." It is completely free — you will never be asked to pay.

2
Create your account

Enter your email and create a password. Verify your email before moving on.

3
Fill out your household profile

Enter your household size and total annual income. This determines which lotteries you're eligible for. Be accurate — you'll verify it with documents later.

4
Browse open lotteries

Go to "Find a Home" to see all open applications. Filter by borough, bedroom size, and income level. Each listing shows income requirements and the application deadline.

5
Apply to every lottery you qualify for

There is no penalty for applying to multiple lotteries at once. Apply to as many as you're eligible for. Each application takes about 2 minutes once your profile is set up.

6
Log in monthly and keep your profile updated

Check your status regularly. If your income or household size changes, update your profile immediately — outdated info can disqualify you even after you've won.

How the Lottery Works

When a developer builds housing using government subsidies, they must set aside affordable units filled through a public lottery on Housing Connect. All entries are selected randomly by computer — applying early offers no advantage. The key is applying to as many lotteries as possible. Income is measured as a percentage of AMI (Area Median Income) — each lottery lists the AMI range per unit type.

What Happens After You Win a Log Number
1
You receive a random log number

All applicants get one. Lower numbers get called first as units become available.

2
The building contacts you for an interview

This can happen months or years later. When they reach out — respond immediately.

3
Income and document verification

You'll need pay stubs, tax returns, bank statements, and ID. Not everyone who wins a log number passes this stage.

4
Criminal background check

Policies vary. NYC's Fair Chance Housing law requires individual review — automatic denials are illegal. If denied, request the reason in writing.

5
Sign the lease and move in

If approved, you sign at the affordable rent rate. You're in.

⚡ What They Don't Tell You
  • The lottery is truly random — applying on day one is no better than applying on the last day. But applying to more lotteries significantly improves your odds.
  • Winning a log number does not mean you won an apartment. The interview, verification, and background check stages filter many people out. Prepare your documents before you even get called.
  • Some buildings have "community preference" — 50% of units go to people in that Community Board district. Apply to buildings near where you're staying to take advantage of this.
  • After you receive a log number, you may hear nothing for years. That is normal. Keep your profile updated so you don't miss the call.
Pros
  • Completely free to apply
  • New buildings, modern amenities
  • Available across all five boroughs
  • No mandatory services
  • Can apply to unlimited lotteries at once
Cons
  • Thousands apply per unit — very competitive
  • Winning a log number doesn't guarantee an apartment
  • Criminal record can disqualify at background check
  • Must keep profile updated or miss your call
  • Long wait even after being selected

What Is Mitchell-Lama?

Mitchell-Lama is a state and city-supervised affordable housing program created in the 1950s to build middle-income housing across New York City. Landlords in the program cannot charge market-rate rents — rents are government-regulated and significantly below market. There are approximately 100 Mitchell-Lama developments still active across all five boroughs.

Unlike Housing Connect, Mitchell-Lama buildings each maintain their own individual waitlists. You apply directly to each building separately. Many are managed through NYC Mitchell-Lama Connect.

How to Apply
  • Visit the Mitchell-Lama Connect site to see which buildings have open waitlists
  • Each building has its own income limits and household size requirements
  • Fill out a separate application for each building you want to apply to
  • There is usually a small application fee of $25–$75 per building
  • Save copies of all applications and confirmation numbers
  • Update your contact info with each building whenever you move
⏱ Wait 3 – 10+ years per building
⚡ What They Don't Tell You
  • Mitchell-Lama waitlists open and close unpredictably. Check the Mitchell-Lama Connect site every few months — new openings come and go without much announcement.
  • Some waitlists have not moved in years because residents pay protected low rents and never leave. Before paying the application fee, ask building management how active their waitlist has been recently.
  • There are both rental and co-op Mitchell-Lama buildings. Rentals are the better option when starting over — co-ops require a down payment most returning citizens do not have immediate access to.
  • Once you are in a Mitchell-Lama rental, your rent can only increase by a small government-regulated percentage each year. The protection is real and lasting.
Pros
  • Significantly below-market rent
  • Government-protected rent increases
  • No mandatory services
  • Some buildings in desirable neighborhoods
  • Long-term housing stability once you're in
Cons
  • Very long and unpredictable waitlists
  • Must apply separately to each building
  • Small fees per application add up
  • Some buildings are leaving the program
  • Co-op buildings require a down payment

💼 NYC Employment Resources

Finding Work After Incarceration in NYC

The difference between someone who struggles for months and someone who gets employed fast is usually one thing: knowing where to go first and walking in with urgency.

The Reality

The Hunger Gap

People who have been home for a while get comfortable — they forget what it felt like to be hungry. People who just came home from incarceration have something most job seekers don't: urgency. You need income and you need it now. That urgency, channeled in the right direction, is your edge.

The mistake most returning citizens make is letting the system slow them down with groups, workshops, and certifications they may not need. The right organizations will cut through all of that and get you to a paycheck. Know which doors to walk through and what to say when you get there.

Common Sense Resources

Two organizations that actually move at the speed you need. No fluff — but there are things you absolutely need to know going in.

Workforce1 — Bond Street Career Center
First Call
What Is It?

Workforce1 (WF1) is NYC's publicly funded workforce development network. The Bond Street Career Center in Brooklyn is one of the most useful locations for returning citizens. They connect job seekers with real employers across industries and are one of the few organizations that move at the speed you actually need right now. The specific program you want inside WF1 is called Employment Works. Ask for it by name the moment you walk in.

⚡ What They Don't Tell You
  • People who have been home a while get comfortable and forget how to be hungry. You just came home — that hunger is your edge. Walk in with urgency and use it. Nobody in that building is as motivated as you are right now.
  • WF1 is not a social services organization. They are not going to try to sell you on groups, counseling sessions, or certifications you may not need. Their job is to get you employed. Let them do it.
  • Say the words "Employment Works" when you walk in. If you don't ask for it specifically, you may end up in a general intake track that moves much slower.
  • Employment Works is built for speed. If you show up ready — with your ID and a sense of what you're willing to do — you can have a real job lead within days, not months. Money, quick.
Pros & Cons
Pros

  • No unnecessary groups or certifications required
  • Real employer connections and job leads
  • Publicly funded — completely free
Cons
  • Must ask for Employment Works specifically or you'll get the slower track
  • Some placements are entry-level — manage expectations
  • Walk-in wait times can be long during peak hours
  • Results depend heavily on your own follow-through
The Fortune Society — Career Development & TWP
Navigate Carefully
What Is It?

The Fortune Society is a large nonprofit serving returning citizens across housing, employment, mental health, and education. They have a lot going on — and not all of it is worth your time. There is, however, a specific path inside Fortune that leads to real employment. Stay focused on it and ignore the noise around it.

The Path That Works
Enroll in Career Development Workshop
→
Complete 3-week workshop
→
Apply for TWP internship immediately after
→
Fortune places you with external employer
→
💰 Income + Experience
Program Details
  • Career Development Workshop: 3 weeks long, Monday–Friday
  • No pay during the workshop — plan your finances before enrolling
  • After the workshop, apply immediately for the Transitional Work Program (TWP) internship
  • Fortune will find and place you with an external employer through TWP
  • Location: Long Island City, Queens
⚠️ What They Don't Tell You
  • Fortune has a lot of programs and a lot of people who will try to direct you toward things you don't need. Stay locked in on Career Development → TWP internship. That is the path. Everything else is a distraction.
  • Do not let them convince you that they are going to hire you. Fortune Society only hires approximately 0.5% of program participants — and getting an internship there in the first place is difficult in itself. That is not the goal.
  • The goal is the TWP internship placement with an external employer — not a job at Fortune. Keep your eyes on that target from day one.
  • The 3-week workshop pays nothing. Have a plan for rent, food, and transportation before you start. Showing up stressed about money makes it harder to get what you need out of the program.
  • The moment you complete the workshop, apply for TWP. Don't wait a week to think about it — momentum matters and placement spots fill up.
Pros & Cons
Pros
  • Structured path leads to real employer placement
  • TWP internship builds experience and references
  • Established employer relationships across industries
  • Free program
  • Internship host can become permanent employer
Cons
  • 3-week workshop pays nothing — plan ahead
  • Large org with a lot of programs — easy to get sidetracked
  • Internship placement through TWP is not guaranteed
  • Fortune almost never hires participants directly (~0.5%)
  • Slower path to income than going directly to WF1

If you're returning from incarceration, your federal financial aid eligibility may have been restored under recent law changes — CCF knows this cold and can walk you through it even if you're not ready for school yet. Ask specifically about this from day one. Also: the peer navigators (current or former students) are more useful than the administrative staff — ask to be connected to them first.
